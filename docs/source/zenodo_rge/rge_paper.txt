Abstract
We introduce Recursive Geometric Entropy (RGE), a specialized application of Recursive Entropy Calculus to geometric systems, establishing fundamental connections between
geometric structure and information content through recursive subdivision. Building on the
general REC framework, we derive concrete results for specific shape classes. For geometryrespecting radial refinements, we prove that entropy grows with a universal slope C = 1
independent of shape, while the shape-dependent offset hΩ serves as a geometric invariant. We derive closed-form expressions for standard shapes (disk: h2 ≈ −0.279, sphere:
h2 ≈ −0.623, cone: h2 ≈ −0.180) and explain the observed transient “1.25 resonance” as
a finite-depth phenomenon. For congruent self-similar subdivisions, we establish C = log2 b
where b is the branching factor, revealing deep connections between symmetry and information growth through prime factorizations. Computational validation achieves O(1/d)
convergence using analytical methods. Applications span computational geometry, machine
learning, materials science, and financial mathematics.
Keywords: recursive entropy, geometric information theory, shape invariants, subdivision algorithms, differential entropy, symmetry groups, computational geometry, informationtheoretic shape analysis, hierarchical partitioning, multiscale entropy

Introduction

The relationship between geometric structure and information content is fundamental to numerous fields, from data compression and machine learning to statistical mechanics and quantum
information theory. While Shannon entropy [2] quantifies uncertainty in discrete probability
distributions, and differential entropy extends this to continuous distributions, there exists no
systematic framework for understanding how geometric subdivision strategies encode information.
This work builds upon the foundational Recursive Entropy Calculus (REC) framework [1],
which established rigorous bounds and resonance phenomena in hierarchically partitioned systems. While REC provides general theoretical machinery for analyzing recursive entropy structures, the present paper specializes this framework to geometric refinements, deriving shapespecific results and connecting information growth to symmetry theory.
We address this gap by introducing Recursive Geometric Entropy (RGE), a specialized
application of Recursive Entropy Calculus [1] to geometric systems. Our key contributions are:
1. Specialization of REC to radial and congruent geometric refinements
2. A unified asymptotic law S(d) = C · d + hΩ + o(1) governing entropy growth at depth d

3. Proof that radial shell refinements yield a universal slope C = 1 for all shapes
4. Demonstration that congruent subdivisions produce shape-specific slopes C = log2 b
5. Closed-form expressions for geometric invariants hΩ of standard shapes
6. Connection between prime factorization and geometric symmetry groups
7. Explanation of finite-depth transient phenomena through asymptotic analysis
8. Computational protocol achieving O(1/d) convergence without Monte Carlo noise

1.1

Related Work

Information theory and geometry have been connected through various lenses. Rényi entropy
[3] and Tsallis entropy [4] generalize Shannon entropy. Geometric information theory [5] studies
statistical manifolds. Fractal geometry [6] relates dimension to information scaling. Multiscale
entropy analysis [7] examines hierarchical structures, and spatial data structures [8] use recursive
decompositions.
The Recursive Entropy Calculus (REC) framework [1] provides the theoretical foundation for
this work, establishing general bounds and resonance phenomena in hierarchically partitioned
systems. RGE specializes REC to geometric domains, deriving concrete results for specific
shape classes and connecting information growth to symmetry theory. While REC provides the
abstract machinery, RGE offers computable geometric invariants and practical applications.

Conclusion

We have introduced Recursive Geometric Entropy as a unified mathematical framework connecting geometric structure to information content. Our main results include:
1. Universal slope C = 1 for radial refinements
2. Shape-specific offsets encoding geometric mass distribution
3. Slope-symmetry correspondence via prime factorizations
4. Explanation of finite-depth transient phenomena
5. Computational protocol with O(1/d) convergence
RGE provides a new lens for analyzing geometric systems across mathematics, physics, computer science, and engineering. Future work includes rigorous treatment of boundary conditions,
extension to fractal geometries, and exploration of quantum geometric entropy.

Discussion

10.1

Theoretical Implications

The universal slope C = 1 for radial refinements represents a fundamental invariant of geometric
information theory, analogous to the speed of light in physics. Regardless of the shape—whether
disk, sphere, cone, or any body of revolution—the entropy growth rate per subdivision depth is
exactly 1 bit. This universality suggests deep connections to:
Thermodynamics: The radial slope may relate to entropy production in spatially extended
systems. The shape-dependent offset hΩ could correspond to configurational entropy.

8

Quantum Information: For quantum systems on curved spaces, RGE might provide
geometric corrections to entanglement entropy. The offset hΩ could encode information about
geometry accessible to local observers.
Black Hole Physics: The area law for black hole entropy (S ∝ A) might find analogs in
RGE. Radial shells near event horizons could exhibit modified entropy scaling.

10.2

The Prime Factorization Pattern

The discovery that entropy slopes decompose as C =
log2 (pi ) for prime factors pi establishes
an unexpected bridge between number theory and geometric information. This suggests:
Group Theory: Prime factors correspond to irreducible symmetries. The number 6 =
2×3 combines binary (reflection) and ternary (rotation) symmetries.
Crystallography: Crystal systems have characteristic branching factors reflecting their
point groups. The 230 space groups should map to specific entropy slopes.
Computational Complexity: Algorithms with branching factor b have complexity O(bd ).
The entropy slope log2 b provides an information-theoretic measure of algorithmic complexity.

10.3

Limitations and Caveats

Regularity Assumptions: Our proofs assume piecewise continuous densities f (x). Shapes
with fractal boundaries or singular densities require extended analysis.
Boundary Effects: The cone density vanishes at boundaries, creating technical issues.
More sophisticated measure theory may be needed.
Mixed Refinement Independence: We assume orthogonal refinements are independent.
Coupled refinements (e.g., radial-angular interactions in rotating systems) need careful treatment.
Computational Limitations: Exact calculations are feasible to depth d ∼ 15. Beyond
this, numerical precision issues emerge. Monte Carlo methods may be necessary for very deep
hierarchies.

10.4

Open Questions

Universality: Does C = 1 hold for all radial refinements, or only specific density classes?
What minimal regularity conditions are necessary?
Optimal Subdivision: For a given computational budget, what subdivision strategy minimizes entropy (maximizes information extraction)? This has applications in adaptive mesh
refinement.
Quantum Extension: How does RGE generalize when probabilities are replaced by quantum amplitudes? Does geometric structure affect quantum entanglement in predictable ways?
Higher Dimensions: Our conjecture hd = − log2 d + (d − 1)/(d ln 2) for d-dimensional
spheres needs rigorous proof. Does it extend to general d-dimensional manifolds?
Continuous Limits: What happens as d → ∞? Does RGE connect to differential entropy
on smooth manifolds?

10.7

Philosophical Considerations

RGE suggests that geometry itself is a form of information storage. The shape of an object
determines how efficiently information about it can be encoded and extracted. This resonates
with Wheeler’s ”it from bit” hypothesis—that physical reality emerges from information.
The universality of C = 1 for radial refinements hints at deep principles governing how
space encodes information. Just as the light cone structure of spacetime is universal, perhaps
the information structure has universal features independent of specific geometry.
The connection between prime factorization and symmetry reveals that even pure mathematics (number theory) and physics (geometric symmetries) are linked through information
theory. This supports a view of mathematics as the study of patterns, with information theory
as the fundamental language.
